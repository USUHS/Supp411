Is the label Fact of Fiction? The Supplement 411 High Risk List and the FDA Health Fraud websites have many examples of products where the label and contents don’t match. You can’t always rely on the label.
Some products that list acacia rigidula actually contain the prohibited stimulant beta-methylphenethylamine. Consumers should be aware of products that promise performance enhancement but list plant-based ingredients on the label. The company may be using the plant name to disguise another substance.
b-methylphenethylamine, N,N-dimethylphenethylamine, geranium, norcoclaurine and methylsynephrine are all prohibited stimulants.
Companies often add a lengthy health warning to protect themselves from liability. If you see a health warning like this one, that is a red flag.
Blends don’t have to list the amount of each ingredient, therefore you can’t determine how much of an ingredient you are getting. Supplement companies often create blends with small amounts of desirable (and expensive) ingredients, and use cheap fillers for the majority.
Guarana, kola nut, and green tea are all sources of caffeine. Citrus aurantium is a plant source of octopamine, and Ma huang is a plant source of ephedrine and pseudoephedrine. These are all prohibited stimulants.
